What is EPR?
▾
The core idea
  • If you manufacture, sell, or import packaging — you pay fees that fund recycling infrastructure.
  • Fees are weight-based: the more packaging you put on the market, the more you pay.
  • A Producer Responsibility Organization (PRO) — typically the Circular Action Alliance (CAA) in the US — collects fees and manages the programme.
  • Fees are eco-modulated: recyclable, recycled-content, or lightweight packaging pays less.
  • The official US Producer Responsibility Organization: Circular Action Alliance (CAA) ↗
Who is a "producer"?
  • Brand owners — companies that sell packaged products under their own brand.
  • Licensees — companies that licence a brand and sell packaged products under it.
  • Importers — companies that import packaged goods where no US brand owner exists.
  • Restaurants, non-profits, small businesses (<$5M revenue) are typically exempt.

How2Recycle (H2R) — Label Redesign & SB 343 Regulations to track — Oct 2026
▾
What changed and why
  • How2Recycle redesigned its Store Drop-Off (SDO) label — the chasing arrows symbol has been removed and replaced with a store drop-off bin icon with clearer action-based instructions.
  • Driven by California's SB 343 "Truth in Recycling" law, which restricts use of chasing arrows on packaging that is not genuinely widely recyclable.
  • New label builds on consumer research showing symbols are the most influential factor in how consumers interpret recyclability labels.
  • Preferred language going forward: "Designed for Store Drop-Off Recyclability" rather than generic "recyclable".
  • See official label artwork and transition resources: How2Recycle Pro Label ↗  |  Store Drop-Off Label ↗

Oregon — Smart Labelling (SB 123) 5-year grace period from Jul 2025
▾
  • Oregon's SB 123 (2023) mandates that manufacturers label products to indicate whether and where they can be recycled in Oregon.
  • Only packaging on the USCL or PRO Recycling Acceptance List may carry a recyclable claim or chasing arrows.
  • For all other packaging, any recycling symbol must be clearly negated (e.g. crossed out) to show it is not recyclable in Oregon.
  • Producers have a 5-year grace period from July 1, 2025 to transition to smart labelling.
  • By Dec 31, 2027, the PRO must recommend how producers can use digital labels (e.g. QR codes) for accurate recyclability communication.
  • Final rules to be confirmed by 2027 — monitor Oregon DEQ updates ↗.
Compostable Labelling — Regulatory Gap & SB 54 Conflict Monitor closely
▾
⚠ The core tension: California's SB 54 requires all single-use plastic packaging to be recyclable or compostable by 2032. But California's AB 1201 requires "compostable" packaging to qualify as an allowable agricultural input under the USDA National Organic Program (NOP) — a standard the USDA hasn't updated to include most compostable packaging. In practice, very little compostable packaging on the market qualifies. This leaves producers caught between two conflicting requirements.
California — AB 1201 (effective June 30, 2027)
  • Products labelled "compostable" or "home compostable" must qualify as allowable agricultural organic input under USDA NOP.
  • Most compostable packaging on the market today does not meet this standard.
  • Grace period runs to June 30, 2027 — monitor USDA and CalRecycle decisions closely before then.
  • BPI certification: currently not an issue, but should be monitored as USDA NOP standards evolve.
Colorado — Compostables Labeling Act SB23-253 (effective July 1, 2024)
  • Products must only be labelled compostable if certified to ASTM D6400 or ASTM D6868 standards.
  • Must be immediately distinguishable — must display a certification logo, the word "compostable", and green symbols or design patterns.
  • Must not display a chasing arrows resin identification code or recycling symbol.
  • Producers of non-certified products are prohibited from using labelling that implies compostability.
Oregon — Compostability study (ongoing)
  • Oregon DEQ is conducting a study on whether covered products safely break down in industrial composting without harming facilities or finished compost quality.
  • Could lead to stricter criteria for what qualifies as compostable, potential bans on problematic materials, and clearer labelling standards.
  • No final rules yet — monitor DEQ updates as study conclusions are published.
What to monitor:
  • USDA NOP updates on compostable standards (critical for California AB 1201)
  • CalRecycle guidance on which materials qualify as compostable under SB 54
  • Oregon DEQ compostability study outcomes
  • Any state-by-state expansion of certified compostable requirements
